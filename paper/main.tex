\begin{abstract}
Most sim-to-real systems adapt policies, simulator parameters, randomized-domain
distributions, domain statistics, or latent context variables. This paper studies
a smaller but deployment-critical coordinate: the hidden failure mechanism that
determines the repair. We formalize repair-equivalence classes and show a simple
aliasing boundary: if two target domains share the same observed statistic but
require margin-separated incompatible repairs, any statistic-only adapter must
fail on at least one of them. We then instantiate mechanism-first adaptation in a
runnable contact-control simulator with actuator gain loss, actuation delay,
high-command slip, and contact compliance under nuisance-heavy shifts. Short
diagnostic interventions produce residual signatures, classify the target into a
repair-equivalence class, and select a controller repair. A full-scale
simulation suite covers 1,151 compact aggregate rows and 756,750 represented
method-domain evaluations across main comparisons, probe-noise taxonomies,
probe-set ablations, mechanism-imbalance sweeps, passive-summary richness,
data-scale controls, repair-library ablations, out-of-library mechanisms,
threshold sensitivity, and constructive counterexamples. In the main sweep,
mechanism-first repair matches the mechanism and best-repair oracles with mean
final error 0.0018, while no adaptation has 0.0945, nuisance nearest neighbor has
0.1014, gain-only system identification has 0.0546, a coarse passive statistic
has 0.0073, and a fine passive statistic has 0.0026. The result is not hardware
evidence and is not a universal argument against randomization, system
identification, or learned representations. It is a constructive boundary case:
when repair-critical mechanisms are aliased by the chosen deployment statistic,
the adaptation coordinate itself matters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Sim-to-real transfer is often framed as a coverage or alignment problem. A robot
is trained under broad domain randomization, the simulator is calibrated against
target observations, source and target features are aligned, or a residual policy
is adapted until deployment behavior improves \citep{tobin2017domain,
peng2018dynamics,tan2018agile,yu2017uposi,chebotar2019closing,
muratore2022robot}. These coordinates are useful and often necessary. They are
also not always the coordinate that the deployment decision requires. A robot
approaching a target contact may not need the complete target-domain
distribution. It may need to know whether to compensate low actuator gain,
predict through delay, avoid high commands that induce slip, preload a compliant
contact, or declare that the target failure is out of the repair library.

This paper studies that distinction. The central claim is that some transfer
failures are caused not by weak optimization over a chosen statistic, but by
choosing a statistic that is not sufficient for repair selection. A domain
embedding may be good for recognizing appearance, a nominal rollout statistic may
be good for estimating average task difficulty, and a simulator parameter vector
may be useful for physical interpretation. None of these is guaranteed to
separate the mechanisms that imply different controller repairs.

We therefore use a repair-equivalence coordinate. Domains are grouped together
when the same repair makes the controller work, even if their nuisance variables,
appearance features, or exact physical parameters differ. Conversely, domains
with similar passive observations are separated when they require incompatible
repairs. The adapter first probes the target with short diagnostic actions,
observes residual signatures, classifies the repair-equivalence class, and then
selects a repair. This is a modest closed-library setting. It is not intended to
solve open-world robot diagnosis. Its purpose is to expose a sharp boundary that
is easy to miss when adaptation is described only as distribution matching.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The paper makes five contributions.
\begin{itemize}
    \item It defines repair-equivalence classes and repair aliasing for
    sim-to-real adaptation.
    \item It proves a small lower-bound proposition: when a statistic aliases
    two domains with different margin-separated optimal repairs, any adapter
    observing only that statistic incurs regret.
    \item It implements a runnable mechanism-first contact-control simulator
    with four hidden failure mechanisms and nuisance-heavy domain variables.
    \item It reports a full-scale evidence suite with main baselines, stronger
    passive summaries, nearest-neighbor comparators, noise taxonomies, probe
    ablations, imbalance controls, data-scale sweeps, repair-library ablations,
    out-of-library controls, threshold sensitivity, and formal counterexamples.
    \item It gives an explicit real-robot audit protocol and falsification
    criteria so the simulation result is not overclaimed as hardware evidence.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Scope.}
The evidence supports a narrow thesis: if the deployment statistic collapses
repair-critical mechanisms, adapting over that statistic is the wrong target. The
evidence does not show that mechanism-first probing is always better than domain
randomization or system identification. Indeed, the experiments include cases
where richer passive summaries and probe-nearest-neighbor baselines match the
mechanism-first rule in this stylized simulator. That is a useful outcome rather
than a problem for the paper: it clarifies that the mechanism-first contribution
is a coordinate claim, not a claim of universal algorithmic dominance.

\section{Problem Setting}

Let $d \in \mathcal{D}$ denote a target deployment domain. A controller must
select a repair $r \in \mathcal{R}$ before or during deployment. The loss
$L(d,r)$ measures task error, safety cost, or negative reward after repair $r$ is
applied in domain $d$. The best available repair is
\[
r^\star(d) \in \arg\min_{r \in \mathcal{R}} L(d,r).
\]
We focus on the finite-repair case because many practical robot adaptation
systems have a bounded menu of interventions: change impedance, reduce speed,
increase force, add delay compensation, use a slip-safe controller, or abort.

\begin{definition}[Repair-equivalence]
Two domains $d_i,d_j$ are repair-equivalent under loss $L$ and repair set
$\mathcal{R}$ if
\[
\arg\min_{r\in \mathcal{R}} L(d_i,r)
= \arg\min_{r\in \mathcal{R}} L(d_j,r).
\]
A mechanism-first adapter estimates this equivalence class or its repair label,
not necessarily the full physical parameter vector.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Repair aliasing]
Let $\sigma(d)$ be a statistic available to an adapter. A pair
$(d_a,d_b)$ is repair-aliased by $\sigma$ if $\sigma(d_a)=\sigma(d_b)$ but
$r^\star(d_a) \neq r^\star(d_b)$.
\end{definition}

The statistic $\sigma$ may be a hand-designed summary, an image feature, a
nominal rollout outcome, an inferred simulator parameter, or a learned context
embedding. The repair-aliasing definition does not say that $\sigma$ is useless.
It only says that $\sigma$ is not sufficient for the specific decision of
selecting a repair from $\mathcal{R}$.

\begin{proposition}[Statistic-only repair aliasing]
Assume there exist two domains $d_a,d_b$ and two distinct repairs $r_a,r_b$ such
that $\sigma(d_a)=\sigma(d_b)$, $r_a$ is the unique optimal repair for $d_a$,
$r_b$ is the unique optimal repair for $d_b$, and every non-optimal repair has
regret at least $\Delta>0$ in its domain. Then any deterministic adapter
$g(\sigma(d))$ incurs regret at least $\Delta$ on either $d_a$ or $d_b$. Any
randomized adapter that conditions only on $\sigma(d)$ incurs expected regret at
least $\Delta/2$ under the uniform distribution over $\{d_a,d_b\}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\sigma(d_a)=\sigma(d_b)$, a deterministic statistic-only adapter returns
the same repair for both domains. If it returns $r_a$, it is non-optimal on
$d_b$ and has regret at least $\Delta$; if it returns $r_b$, it is non-optimal on
$d_a$; and any other repair is non-optimal on both. For a randomized adapter, let
$p_a$ and $p_b$ be the probabilities assigned to $r_a$ and $r_b$ after observing
the shared statistic. The expected regret is at least
\[
\frac{\Delta}{2}(1-p_a) + \frac{\Delta}{2}(1-p_b)
= \Delta\left(1-\frac{p_a+p_b}{2}\right).
\]
Because $r_a\neq r_b$, $p_a+p_b\leq 1$, so the expected regret is at least
$\Delta/2$.
\end{proof}

The proposition is deliberately simple. Its role is to identify a boundary that
can be hidden inside empirical adaptation pipelines. If the chosen observation
coordinate loses the distinction required for repair, no amount of optimization
over that coordinate can recover it.

\section{Mechanism-First Repair Coordinates}

The mechanism-first adapter assumes a closed library of repairable mechanisms
$\mathcal{M}$ and repairs $\mathcal{R}$. Each mechanism $m\in\mathcal{M}$ is
associated with a diagnostic residual signature under a small set of probe
actions. The adapter executes probes, maps the observed residuals to a mechanism
label, and applies the corresponding repair:
\[
d \xrightarrow{\mathrm{probe}} \phi(d) \xrightarrow{h} \hat{m}
\xrightarrow{\rho} r .
\]
Here $\phi(d)$ is not a passive domain summary. It is an intervention response
chosen because the candidate mechanisms should separate under it. In the
simulator, low pulses, high pulses, and alternating pulses distinguish gain
loss, delay, high-command slip, and compliance.

\paragraph{Not full system identification.}
Mechanism-first adaptation can be coarser than system identification. A gain of
0.49 and a gain of 0.61 may be different physical parameters but the same repair
class if both require gain compensation. Conversely, two domains can have nearly
identical passive rollout summaries but require different repairs because one is
delayed and another slips under high commands. The target variable is the repair
coordinate, not the complete dynamics model.

\paragraph{Closed-library assumption.}
The mechanism-first rule is useful only when the repair library contains an
appropriate response. The experiments therefore include repair-library ablations
and mixed mechanisms. These negative controls are central. If the correct repair
is removed, mechanism classification can remain accurate while repair quality
falls. If the target mechanism is out of library, a confident in-library label
can be harmful unless a rejection mechanism works.

\section{Simulator}

The experiment is a one-dimensional contact-control task. A controller pushes an
object from position 0 to target position 1 within eight control steps. Each
deployment domain samples one hidden mechanism and nuisance variables. The
controller observes neither the mechanism label nor the true parameters.

\paragraph{Mechanisms.}
The four in-library mechanisms are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Actuator gain loss:} the executed displacement is a reduced
    multiple of the commanded action.
    \item \textbf{One-step actuation delay:} the displacement at time $t$ uses
    the previous command.
    \item \textbf{High-command slip:} commands above a threshold lose effective
    displacement.
    \item \textbf{Contact compliance:} early commands compress the contact
    before productive motion begins.
\end{itemize}
Each domain also carries nuisance texture, lighting, appearance, and sensor-noise
variables. These variables are intentionally not repair-determining, so a
nuisance-heavy nearest-neighbor baseline is expected to struggle.

\paragraph{Repairs.}
The repair library contains no adaptation, gain compensation, delay-aware
braking, slip-safe low-amplitude commands, and preload for compliant contact.
The best-repair oracle chooses the highest-reward repair per domain. The
mechanism oracle maps the true mechanism to its intended repair. The proposed
mechanism-first adapter maps diagnostic residuals to the repair label.

\paragraph{Diagnostic probes.}
The probe set consists of low pulses, high pulses, and alternating pulses. Low
pulses identify reduced gain and compliant dead zones. High pulses expose delay
and slip. Alternating pulses test whether a residual is directionally symmetric.
Probe features include first displacement, total displacement, second-step jump,
high-to-low ratio, and alternating residuals. The full-scale suite also tests
what happens when each probe group is removed or corrupted.

\section{Experimental Design}

The full-scale runner writes compact aggregate rows only. It does not store raw
episode traces except for small constructive counterexample artifacts. This keeps
RAM and disk use low while allowing many conditions to be evaluated.

\paragraph{Main methods.}
The main comparison includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item no adaptation;
    \item random repair;
    \item the best single robust repair learned from training domains;
    \item coarse, medium, and fine passive-statistic tables;
    \item passive-vector nearest neighbor;
    \item nuisance nearest neighbor over passive and appearance variables;
    \item probe nearest neighbor;
    \item gain-only system identification;
    \item mechanism-first probing;
    \item mechanism oracle;
    \item best-repair oracle.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Suites.}
The final evidence includes 11 aggregate suites: main sweep, by-mechanism
breakdown, probe-noise taxonomy, probe-set ablation, mechanism mixture and
imbalance, passive-summary richness, data-scale sensitivity, repair-library
ablation, unknown/mixed mechanisms, threshold sensitivity, and formal
counterexamples. The generated summary reports 1,151 compact rows, 756,750
represented method-domain evaluations, 101 artifact metadata rows, and 20
constructive passive-aliasing counterexample rows.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
We report thresholded success, mean final error, reward, regret to the
best-repair oracle, repair accuracy, mechanism accuracy where applicable, and
unknown-rejection rate for rejection methods. The thresholded success metric is
useful but not sufficient: several passive methods reach high success while
still having worse final error or repair accuracy.

Table~\ref{tab:main} and Figure~\ref{fig:main-methods} show the main result.
Mechanism-first repair has mean final error 0.0018, zero measured regret to the
best-repair oracle, and perfect mechanism and repair accuracy in the clean
closed-library simulator. No adaptation has final error 0.0945. Nuisance nearest
neighbor has final error 0.1014 and repair accuracy 0.294, confirming that
appearance-heavy similarity is not a reliable repair coordinate. Gain-only
system identification has final error 0.0546 because it detects gain loss but
does not identify delay, slip, or compliance.

The passive baselines are more nuanced. The coarse passive statistic reaches
success 1.000 but has final error 0.0073 and repair accuracy 0.506. The fine
passive statistic improves to final error 0.0026 and repair accuracy 0.900. A
medium passive statistic and a passive-vector nearest-neighbor baseline nearly
match the mechanism-first method in this simulator. This is an important
boundary condition: the mechanism-first rule is not claiming that passive
features are always insufficient. The claim is that passive features are
insufficient when they alias repair-critical mechanisms. Rich enough passive
vectors can sometimes reconstruct the same repair partition, especially in a
simple low-dimensional simulator.

\begin{figure}[t]

\caption{Mechanism accuracy under five corruption families. Gaussian noise and

\label{fig:noise}
\end{figure}

The strongest limitation of mechanism-first adaptation is probe reliability.
With Gaussian feature noise at level 0.02, mechanism accuracy is 0.979 and final
error is 0.0031. At level 0.04, accuracy falls to 0.828 and final error rises to
0.0320. At level 0.12, accuracy is 0.508 and final error is 0.1106. Dropout
shows a similar but smoother degradation: at dropout probability 0.20,
mechanism accuracy is 0.820 and final error is 0.0449. Sign flips at probability
0.20 yield accuracy 0.819 and final error 0.0432. Bias is asymmetric: small
biases are harmless, but large signed biases collapse the rule because they move
multiple pulse features across classifier thresholds. Quantization is benign at
the tested levels because the signatures remain well separated.

These results narrow the claim. Mechanism-first repair is only as good as its
diagnostic interventions. If probes are noisy, unsafe, too expensive, or
uninformative, a passive or robust method may be preferable. The mechanism-first
view therefore turns probe design into a first-class experimental object.

\section{Passive Summary Richness and Data Scale}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/full_scale_passive_richness.png}
\caption{Passive-summary richness. Coarse passive statistics are insufficient,
but richer passive vectors can recover the same repair partition in this simple
simulator.}
\label{fig:passive}
\end{figure}

The passive-summary suite asks whether the apparent advantage of
mechanism-first repair is only a weak-baseline artifact. The answer is partly
yes and partly no. Coarse passive statistics remain clearly worse, with final
error 0.0073 and repair accuracy about 0.50. Fine and trace passive tables
reduce the error to roughly 0.0025. Passive-vector nearest neighbor matches the
oracle in this simulator because the passive vector contains enough dynamics
information to identify the repair label.

This result is deliberately included because it prevents overclaiming. The
repair-aliasing proposition applies to insufficient statistics. It does not say
that all passive summaries are insufficient. In low-dimensional systems, a rich
passive trajectory can become a diagnostic signature. In more complex contact
settings, however, passive behavior may be unsafe, ambiguous, or confounded by
task policy. The mechanism-first argument is strongest when targeted
interventions reveal distinctions that passive deployment summaries hide.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/full_scale_mechanism_mix.png}

\label{fig:mix}

Mechanism imbalance can make a weak method look strong. For example, if gain
loss dominates the target distribution, a gain-focused adapter may perform well
without solving delay, slip, or compliance. Figure~\ref{fig:mix} therefore
tests uniform, gain-heavy, delay-heavy, slip-heavy, rare-compliance, and
two-mechanism distributions. Mechanism-first and probe nearest neighbor remain
near the oracle. Passive fine summaries are also strong in this simulator but
remain sensitive to coverage and binning. The nuisance baseline remains poor
because nuisance variables are not causally tied to repair.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/full_scale_repair_ablation.png}
\caption{Repair-library ablation. Mechanism labels are not sufficient when the
matching repair is missing; the method then falls back to the best available
repair and incurs regret.}
\label{fig:repair-ablation}
\end{figure}

Repair-library ablations are the most important boundary control. When
delay-brake is removed, mechanism-first limited repair has mean regret 0.0265
and success 0.739, while the best-repair oracle over the reduced library still
finds a partial workaround with final error 0.0066 and success 0.994. When
gain-compensation is removed, mechanism-first regret is 0.0117. Removing preload
is less damaging because other repairs partly handle compliance in this toy
system. Adding a wrong high-gain repair has little effect because the repair
selector does not choose it. These results show that mechanism classification
does not by itself solve adaptation; the repair library must contain useful
actions.

Table~\ref{tab:unknown} reports out-of-library and mixed-mechanism controls.

rejects 0.645 of cases and has lower success than plain mechanism-first.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Domain randomization and robust policies.}
Domain randomization trains policies over visual and dynamics variation so that
the learned behavior generalizes to real deployment \citep{tobin2017domain,
peng2018dynamics,muratore2022robot}. The present paper does not dispute that
strategy. Instead, it identifies a case where randomized coverage of nuisance
factors is not the same as identifying the repair mechanism. Robust policies can
reduce the need for explicit repair selection, but the single-robust baseline
shows that one repair can be dominated when the target mechanisms require
incompatible interventions.

\paragraph{System identification and sim calibration.}
Online system identification and sim calibration estimate physical parameters or
adapt randomization distributions \citep{tan2018agile,yu2017uposi,
chebotar2019closing}. Mechanism-first adaptation is coarser. It only estimates
the repair-equivalence class. That coarseness can be an advantage when the
complete parameter vector is unnecessary, and a disadvantage when the mechanism
is mixed or out of library.

\paragraph{Domain adaptation and contact-rich transfer.}
Domain adaptation methods align source and target observations, including image
translation and force-control pipelines for assembly \citep{shi2023cyclegan,
yuan2022assembly}. Tactile and contact-rich sim-to-real work shows that
interaction signals can be central for transfer \citep{ding2021tactile,
lin2023bitouch}. This paper is closest in spirit to those interaction-focused
approaches, but the claimed contribution is not simply that contact matters. It
is that contact residuals should be organized around the repair they imply.

\section{Real-Robot Audit Protocol}

The next empirical step is a real-robot audit, not another simulator-only
leaderboard. A hardware audit should include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a closed repair library with pre-registered safe command limits;
    \item diagnostic probes whose energy, displacement, and contact forces are
    bounded independently of the classifier;
    \item blind mechanism labels or post-hoc failure annotations;
    \item passive-summary baselines that are allowed rich trajectories, not just
    coarse success/failure bins;
    \item rejection criteria for out-of-library mechanisms;
    \item per-mechanism failure narratives, not only aggregate success;
    \item an ablation in which a required repair is removed from the library.
\end{itemize}
The mechanism-first claim would be weakened if rich passive summaries recover
the same repair partition with equal safety and lower cost, if diagnostic probes
are too noisy or unsafe, or if mixed mechanisms dominate real deployments.

\section{Limitations}

The strongest limitation is that all evidence is simulation evidence. The
simulator is intentionally small and diagnostic. It does not include perception
errors, deformable contact, unmodeled actuator dynamics, force limits, changing
friction, or human safety constraints. The mechanism library is closed and
hand-designed. The probe classifier is also hand-designed in the main method,
although the probe-nearest-neighbor baseline shows that the same coordinate can
be learned in this feature space.

The passive baseline story is nuanced. Coarse passive statistics are
insufficient, but passive-vector nearest neighbor matches the oracle in this
toy domain. This means the paper should not be read as a general defeat of
passive representations. It should be read as a warning: a passive statistic is
only safe as an adaptation coordinate if it is repair-sufficient.

The negative controls also matter. Gain+delay mixed mechanisms break the
closed-library mechanism-first rule. Removing delay-brake from the repair
library produces substantial regret. Probe noise at sufficient magnitude erases
the advantage. These are not side notes; they are the conditions under which the
method should not be deployed without additional diagnosis or fallback logic.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper changes the target of adaptation from domain statistics to repair
mechanisms. The change exposes a simple boundary: statistics that are useful for
domain recognition or nominal rollout prediction can still be insufficient for
repair selection. In a runnable contact-control simulator, mechanism-first
probing matches the best-repair oracle in the clean closed-library setting and
outperforms no adaptation, nuisance similarity, gain-only system identification,
and coarse passive statistics. The broader suite also shows where the claim
ends: rich passive vectors can match the repair coordinate, noisy probes degrade
the method, missing repairs create regret, and mixed mechanisms can fail. The
useful lesson is therefore not that one algorithm wins universally. It is that
sim-to-real adaptation should ask whether its chosen coordinate is sufficient
for the repair decision it must make.

\section{Algorithmic Details}

The mechanism-first adapter has three phases. First, execute a bounded probe
set. In the simulator this consists of low pulses, high pulses, and alternating
pulses. Second, compute residual features: first displacement, total
displacement, jump after a delayed step, high-to-low response ratio, and
alternating residuals. Third, classify the mechanism and select the corresponding
repair. The hand-designed classifier uses threshold tests that correspond to
physical signatures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a near-zero first high response with a large second jump indicates
    delay;
    \item low first response followed by high response indicates compliance;
    \item low response with high-command collapse indicates slip;
    \item reduced but monotone response indicates gain loss.
\end{itemize}

The probe-nearest-neighbor baseline uses the same probe feature vector but
learns labels from training domains. Its strong performance shows that the
coordinate, not only the hand-coded rule, carries repair information.

\section{Simulator Equations}

The nominal controller computes a clipped proportional command
\[
u_t = \mathrm{clip}(0.72(1-x_t), -0.46, 0.46).
\]
Gain compensation divides the nominal command by the expected low gain.
Delay-brake predicts the next state using the previous command. Slip-safe repair
limits command amplitude. Preload sends a large first command to compress the
contact and then returns to feedback control.

The reward is negative final error minus action cost and overshoot cost:
\[
R(d,r) = -|1-x_T| - 0.015\sum_t |u_t| - 0.35\max(0,x_T-1).
\]
Success requires final error below the selected threshold and overshoot below
0.10. The default threshold is 0.07.

\section{Baseline Details}

\paragraph{Passive tables.}
The coarse passive table observes only whether nominal rollout succeeds or
fails. Medium and fine tables add quantized nominal final position, nominal
error, low-pulse sum, and early nominal positions. The trace table adds more
quantized temporal structure. Each table chooses the empirically best repair in
its bin using training domains.

\paragraph{Nearest-neighbor baselines.}
Passive-vector nearest neighbor uses nominal rollout and low-pulse summaries.
Nuisance nearest neighbor adds lighting, texture, and appearance variables.
Probe nearest neighbor uses diagnostic residuals. The contrast between nuisance
nearest neighbor and probe nearest neighbor is the cleanest empirical
demonstration of the coordinate issue: both are nearest-neighbor methods, but
one uses repair-irrelevant nuisance similarity and the other uses
repair-relevant intervention responses.

\paragraph{Oracles.}
The mechanism oracle maps the hidden mechanism label to the intended repair.
The best-repair oracle evaluates the finite repair library and chooses the best
reward for each domain. In the clean in-library simulator these oracles match,
but they can differ in repair-library ablations and mixed mechanisms.

\section{Additional Interpretation of Main Results}

The main result has two readings. The optimistic reading is that a small
mechanism-first probe can match the oracle in a closed-library setting. The
cautious reading is that the simulator is simple enough that passive-vector
nearest neighbor also recovers the repair partition. Both readings are true. The
paper's central point survives because the coarse passive statistic remains
aliased, nuisance similarity remains poor, gain-only identification remains
incomplete, and the formal proposition identifies why insufficient statistics
cannot be fixed by a better optimizer.

The medium passive table is surprisingly strong. This happens because the
one-dimensional task exposes enough mechanism information through nominal
rollout summaries. In a higher-dimensional contact task, nominal trajectories
could be unsafe or ambiguous; in that case active diagnostic probes may be
preferable. The right conclusion is to test repair sufficiency, not to assume a
particular coordinate wins.

\section{Probe Noise Details}

The noise taxonomy uses five corruptions. Gaussian noise perturbs all probe
features independently. Bias applies a shared signed offset to pulse features.
Dropout zeros individual features. Quantization rounds features to a fixed grid.
Sign-flip corruption negates randomly selected features. The taxonomy is useful
because it separates benign measurement loss from adversarial residual changes.
Quantization is benign in the tested range because class thresholds remain
separated. Bias is severe because it moves multiple diagnostic features in a
coordinated direction.

\section{Repair-Library Failure Narratives}

When gain compensation is removed, gain-loss domains are forced to use weaker
repairs. Mechanism labels remain correct, but the repair set lacks the intended
action. When delay-brake is removed, the failure is larger because delayed
dynamics require a qualitatively different controller. When slip-safe repair is
removed, other conservative actions partially cover the slip mechanism in this
toy domain. When preload is removed, compliance is often still manageable
because the controller can eventually compress the contact, though with higher
regret. Adding a wrong high-gain repair is harmless because the repair selector
does not choose it; a learned selector without repair-level validation could
behave differently.

\section{Out-of-Library Controls}

The friction control demonstrates that physical mechanism labels need not match
repair-equivalence labels. Friction is not one of the four in-library mechanisms,
but its best repair in the tested range is equivalent to an in-library response.
The gain+delay control demonstrates the opposite. It combines two mechanisms
whose repairs interact, and the closed-library classifier maps it to a single
label. The result is failure. Slip+compliance is intermediate: the in-library
repairs are often adequate, but the rejection rule is not reliably beneficial.
These controls motivate future work on compositional mechanism labels and
calibrated unknown detection.

\section{Falsification Criteria}

The mechanism-first claim should be weakened or rejected under any of the
following outcomes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a passive statistic available at equal or lower deployment cost is
    repair-sufficient across the target domain;
    \item diagnostic probes cannot be executed safely;
    \item probe residuals are too noisy to classify repair-equivalence classes;
    \item target failures are mostly mixed or out-of-library;
    \item the repair library lacks the actions implied by the detected
    mechanisms;
    \item mechanism labels do not improve regret relative to a robust repair.
\end{itemize}
These criteria are part of the contribution because they prevent the simulation
result from becoming a vague argument for more diagnosis in all settings.

\section{Full-Scale Suite Ledger}

This appendix records what each full-scale suite is meant to test and what kind
of claim it can support. The ledger matters because a large simulator run can
look impressive while answering only one narrow question. The final evidence is
therefore organized around distinct reviewer attacks.

\paragraph{Main sweep.}
The main sweep asks whether the mechanism-first coordinate is competitive with
reasonable alternatives in the clean in-library setting. It is not enough for
mechanism-first to beat no adaptation. The relevant comparison includes a strong
single robust repair, gain-only system identification, passive tables with
increasing resolution, passive-vector nearest neighbor, nuisance nearest
neighbor, probe nearest neighbor, and oracles. The main sweep supports the
closed-library positive claim: diagnostic residuals are sufficient to recover
the intended repair labels in this simulator. It does not support any claim
about out-of-library robustness, real hardware, or learned probe synthesis.

\paragraph{By-mechanism breakdown.}
The by-mechanism rows prevent a method from hiding a failure on one mechanism
behind success on another. This is especially important when the mechanism
mixture is imbalanced. A gain-heavy test distribution can make gain-focused
system identification look better than it is. The by-mechanism figure shows
whether each method actually solves gain, delay, slip, and compliance.

\paragraph{Probe-noise taxonomy.}
The noise taxonomy asks whether the diagnostic coordinate is stable under
measurement corruption. Gaussian noise, bias, dropout, quantization, and sign
flips are qualitatively different. A method that survives quantization may fail
under bias, because quantization preserves ordering while bias can move several
features across thresholds together. The taxonomy supports a conditional claim:
mechanism-first repair is useful when probes remain discriminative.

\paragraph{Probe-set ablation.}
The probe-set ablation asks whether the full probe set is necessary and whether
the result is brittle to a single hand-selected intervention. In this simulator,
low-only and high-only feature sets already separate the mechanisms, while
alternating-only probes are weaker. This result should not be generalized as a
claim that low or high pulses are sufficient in real contact tasks. It says that
the simulated mechanisms are separable by several low-dimensional intervention
views, and that alternating residuals alone are less informative.

\paragraph{Mechanism mixture and imbalance.}
The mixture suite asks whether methods exploit the mechanism prior. A method
that chooses the most common repair can perform well under a skewed prior and
fail under a uniform prior. The suite includes uniform, gain-heavy, delay-heavy,
slip-heavy, rare-compliance, and two-mechanism mixes. The mechanism-first result
is stable because it conditions on probe responses rather than on the prior.

\paragraph{Passive-summary richness.}
The passive-summary suite is intentionally adversarial to the paper's thesis.
It gives passive adapters more information and asks whether the repair partition
can be recovered without active probes. In this simulator, passive-vector nearest
neighbor matches the oracle. That finding is retained because it clarifies the
boundary: the problem is not passivity itself, but repair-insufficient
statistics. If a passive vector is repair-sufficient and cheaper than probing,
it should be used.

\paragraph{Data-scale sensitivity.}
The data-scale suite asks whether passive and nearest-neighbor methods merely
need more training domains. Mechanism-first has little dependence on training
count because it is a hand-coded rule in a repair-aligned feature space.
Passive-statistic tables improve with coverage but remain sensitive to binning.
The scale suite supports a data-efficiency claim only inside the toy simulator;
it does not prove that hand-designed probes will remain data-efficient on
hardware.

\paragraph{Repair-library ablation.}
The repair ablation asks whether mechanism labels remain useful when the repair
menu is incomplete. The answer is mixed. Classification remains meaningful, but
the controller cannot apply a missing repair. This suite is the strongest guard
against an overbroad diagnosis claim. Diagnosis is useful only if the system can
act on the diagnosis.

\paragraph{Unknown and mixed mechanisms.}
The unknown suite distinguishes repair-equivalence from physical taxonomy. A
friction-like target can be out-of-library physically but in-library by repair.
A gain+delay target can be physically understandable but out-of-library by
repair because the library lacks a compositional repair. This suite motivates
future work on hierarchical labels, compositional mechanisms, and calibrated
rejection.

\paragraph{Threshold sensitivity.}
The threshold suite asks whether the conclusion depends on the default success
threshold. It is deliberately secondary to final error and regret, because
thresholded success can saturate. The main use of this suite is to show that the
nominal controller's reported success changes substantially as the threshold is
relaxed, while mechanism-first remains near saturation at the tested strict
thresholds.

\paragraph{Formal counterexamples.}
The counterexample table is not an empirical benchmark. It is a constructive
artifact for Proposition 1. The rows instantiate the logic of two domains with a
shared passive statistic and different best repairs. They make the lower-bound
argument inspectable and reproducible.

\section{Mechanism Signatures and Separability}

Mechanism-first adaptation depends on separability assumptions. In this
simulator, each mechanism changes a different part of the probe response. Gain
loss scales small and large commands. Delay suppresses the first response to a
high command and produces a second-step jump. Slip preserves small commands but
reduces the effect of high commands. Compliance suppresses early motion until
compression is released. These signatures are intentionally simple, but they
capture a common contact-control reality: different physical failures can have
similar task-level outcomes while producing different residuals under targeted
interventions.

The separability assumption has two levels. The first is physical: the true
mechanisms must produce distinguishable residuals under safe probes. The second
is statistical: the measurement system must observe those residuals with enough
signal-to-noise ratio. The probe-noise taxonomy attacks the second level. The
unknown and mixed controls attack the first. A real robot can violate either
assumption. For example, a compliant object with high damping might look like
actuator gain loss under low pulses, and a delayed controller with noisy state
estimation might look like slip under high pulses.

Probe separability also depends on the repair library. If two physical
mechanisms require the same repair, separating them is unnecessary. If one
physical mechanism can require different repairs depending on severity, the
mechanism label is too coarse. The right label is not the name of a physical
phenomenon. It is the repair-equivalence class induced by the controller and
loss.

\section{Passive Sufficiency Versus Active Diagnosis}

The paper's argument can be misunderstood as saying that active probes are
always superior to passive summaries. The experiments explicitly reject that
stronger claim. Passive-vector nearest neighbor matches the oracle in the main
simulator because the passive vector contains enough information to reconstruct
the repair partition. This is not a contradiction. It is exactly what the formal
view predicts: if a statistic is repair-sufficient, a statistic-only adapter can
succeed.

The practical question is therefore not whether a summary is passive or active.
The question is whether it is repair-sufficient at acceptable cost. Passive
summaries can be attractive because they do not require extra interventions.
They can also be dangerous because they may be collected during task execution,
where a wrong action can damage the object or robot. Active probes can be safer
if they are small, bounded, and designed to reveal a mechanism before committing
to a high-energy policy. Active probes can also be worse if they add time,
disturb the scene, or require contact that the task would otherwise avoid.

The repair-sufficiency test suggested by this paper is simple. Choose a
candidate statistic $\sigma$. Search for target domains that share $\sigma$ but
have different best repairs. If such pairs exist with nontrivial margins, the
statistic is unsafe as the sole adaptation coordinate. If no such pairs are
found under a broad audit, the statistic may be sufficient for the repair
library and task distribution. This test can be applied to passive trajectories,
learned embeddings, tactile summaries, or intervention residuals.

\section{Statistical Reporting Choices}

The full-scale artifact stores compact aggregate rows rather than raw traces.
This choice is deliberate. The simulator is deterministic given a sampled domain
and repair, and the main questions are aggregate repair quality, mechanism
accuracy, and regret. Storing every per-step trace for every condition would
increase disk usage without improving the paper's claims. The aggregate rows
retain seed, condition, method, training count, test count, success rate, mean
final error, p95 final error, mean reward, mean regret, repair accuracy,
mechanism accuracy, and unknown-rejection rate where applicable.

The reported standard errors in the JSON summary are across aggregate seed rows,
not across individual time steps. This is the appropriate level for the
experiment because domain samples, not time steps, are the independent units.
The suite-split execution also means some boundary controls have fewer seeds
than the main sweep. Those controls are presented as stress tests and failure
narratives, not as high-precision leaderboard estimates.

The paper emphasizes final error and regret because thresholded success can
saturate. A method with success 1.000 can still have meaningfully worse final
error or repair accuracy. This occurs for coarse passive statistics: the task is
often solved under the loose threshold, but the selected repair is not always
the best repair. For robotics, this distinction matters because small residual
errors can accumulate, cause contact wear, or reduce downstream success even
when a single-step benchmark declares success.

\section{Probe Safety and Deployment Cost}

Diagnostic probes are not free. A useful hardware implementation must bound
probe amplitude, velocity, duration, contact force, and workspace displacement.
The probe should be safer than executing the unadapted task policy. If a probe
can itself damage the scene, the method has merely moved risk earlier in the
pipeline.

A practical probe-design workflow is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item enumerate plausible repair-critical mechanisms for the task;
    \item define a repair library and loss function before designing probes;
    \item choose low-energy interventions that are expected to separate the
    repair classes;
    \item simulate and, where possible, bench-test each probe under worst-case
    contact assumptions;
    \item train or specify a classifier with an explicit unknown region;
    \item evaluate repair regret, not only mechanism-label accuracy;
    \item remove each repair from the library and verify that the system fails
    gracefully;
    \item audit whether a passive statistic can achieve the same repair
    partition with lower cost.
\end{enumerate}
This workflow treats mechanism-first adaptation as an engineering hypothesis,
not as a default recipe.

\section{Compositional Mechanisms}

The gain+delay negative control is the clearest open problem. The target has two
in-library physical mechanisms, but the repair library is not compositional.
Selecting gain compensation alone does not solve delay, and selecting delay
brake alone does not fully solve gain loss. The best-repair oracle over the
available library can find the least bad single repair, but the mechanism-first
classifier is not designed to output a set of mechanisms or a composed
controller.

A compositional extension would replace the single label $\hat{m}$ with a
structured diagnosis such as $\{\mathrm{gain},\mathrm{delay}\}$. The repair map
$\rho$ would then need to compose interventions, for example combining gain
compensation with delay-aware prediction. This is not always safe: repairs can
interact nonlinearly, and composing two stable repairs can produce an unstable
controller. A future compositional version should therefore treat repair
composition as a controlled synthesis problem, not as a simple union of labels.

\section{Unknown Detection}

Unknown detection is harder than classification. The rejection variant in the
experiments uses distance in probe-feature space as a crude unknown score. That
score is insufficient. Friction is out-of-library physically but near an
in-library repair-equivalence class, so rejection is unnecessary. Gain+delay is
near in-library components but out-of-library as a repair composition, so
distance-based rejection can miss it. Slip+compliance shows the opposite
problem: rejection can trigger often without improving task quality.

This suggests that useful rejection should be based on predicted repair regret
or residual uncertainty after a candidate repair, not only on feature-space
distance. A robot can execute a small verification action after selecting a
repair and check whether the residual moves in the expected direction. If not,
the system should reduce authority, request a safer mode, or fall back to a
robust controller. The current paper does not implement that loop; it only
exposes why it is needed.

\section{Reviewer-Facing Claim Boundaries}

Several stronger claims are tempting but unsupported. The paper does not show
that mechanism-first adaptation outperforms modern learned latent-context
policies. It does not show that hand-designed probes scale to high-dimensional
manipulation. It does not show that diagnostic interventions are safe on
hardware. It does not show that passive summaries are generally weak. It does
not show that physical mechanism labels are always identifiable.

The supported claims are narrower:
\begin{itemize}
    \item repair-equivalence is a useful formal coordinate for some sim-to-real
    failures;
    \item statistic-only adapters fail when their statistic aliases
    margin-separated repairs;
    \item in the provided simulator, diagnostic residuals recover the repair
    class under clean in-library conditions;
    \item the advantage degrades under noisy probes, missing repairs, and mixed
    mechanisms;
    \item richer passive summaries can match the repair coordinate when they are
    repair-sufficient.
\end{itemize}
These boundaries make the paper stronger. They turn the contribution from a
broad slogan into a testable statement about adaptation coordinates.

\section{How To Read the Results as a Design Tool}

The results can be used as a design checklist for a new robot task. First, list
the repairs the robot can actually execute. Second, ask which target-domain
differences change the best repair. Third, test whether the available passive
statistics separate those differences. Fourth, design the smallest safe probes
that separate any remaining repair-aliased pairs. Fifth, add negative controls:
remove repairs, corrupt probes, skew mechanism priors, and introduce mixed
mechanisms. If the mechanism-first method only wins in the clean setting and
fails every negative control, the design is not ready for deployment.

This design reading is more important than the specific one-dimensional
simulator. The simulator is a microscope for the coordinate issue. A real robot
will need richer models, better probes, safety envelopes, and learned
uncertainty. The coordinate question remains the same: does the information used
for adaptation distinguish the repairs the robot must choose between?

\section{Method-by-Method Analysis}

\paragraph{Nominal no adaptation.}
The nominal controller measures how much of the task is solved without target
information. Its success rate is not zero because many sampled domains are mild
enough for feedback to reach the target under the default threshold. This is why
the paper reports final error and regret in addition to success. The nominal
controller answers the question: is repair selection unnecessary? In the main
sweep, the answer is no. Its final error is 0.0945 and its regret is 0.1030.

\paragraph{Random repair.}
Random repair estimates how often the repair library contains harmless
interventions. If random repair were close to mechanism-first, the library would
not require diagnosis. Instead, random repair has repair accuracy near chance,
final error 0.0972, and regret 0.1104. The repair menu therefore contains
incompatible actions.

\paragraph{Single robust repair.}
The single robust repair is the best one-size-fits-all repair learned from
training domains. It uses the target mechanism distribution but refuses to
condition on the target instance. It improves over nominal control, but its final
error 0.0213 remains much worse than mechanism-first. This gap is the empirical
signature of repair heterogeneity.

\paragraph{Passive statistic tables.}
The passive tables test increasingly rich statistic-only adaptation. Coarse
statistics are repair-aliased: the task may still succeed, but repair accuracy
is poor. Medium and fine tables are much stronger because they encode more of
the nominal trajectory. This gradient is useful because it shows that the paper
is not merely comparing mechanism-first to a weak passive baseline.

\paragraph{Passive-vector nearest neighbor.}
Passive-vector nearest neighbor matches the oracle in the main simulator. This
is retained as a boundary result. In a low-dimensional deterministic task, a
passive vector can become repair-sufficient. A hardware study should therefore
include rich passive baselines before claiming active probes are necessary.

\paragraph{Nuisance nearest neighbor.}
Nuisance nearest neighbor adds lighting, texture, and appearance. It performs
poorly not because nearest neighbor is weak, but because the similarity
coordinate is wrong. Probe nearest neighbor uses the same basic algorithm and
succeeds. The contrast isolates the adaptation-coordinate issue.

\paragraph{Gain-only system identification.}
Gain-only system identification is a partial mechanism estimator. It detects one
mechanism but ignores delay, slip, and compliance. Its final error 0.0546 shows
the cost of estimating a physical parameter that is useful but not sufficient
for the repair decision.

\paragraph{Probe nearest neighbor.}
Probe nearest neighbor uses diagnostic residuals and learns labels from training
examples. It matches mechanism-first in the clean simulator. This supports the
coordinate claim more than the hand-coded classifier claim: a learned method can
also succeed if its representation is repair-aligned.

\paragraph{Oracles.}
The mechanism oracle maps true mechanism labels to intended repairs. The
best-repair oracle evaluates the repair library per domain. They match in the
clean in-library sweep and diverge in boundary controls, where missing repairs
or mixed mechanisms break the one-label repair map.

\section{Claim-to-Evidence Map}

\paragraph{Repair-equivalence is a meaningful coordinate.}
The main sweep supports this claim because mechanism-first, probe nearest
neighbor, and the mechanism oracle recover the best-repair oracle in the clean
closed-library simulator. The limitation is that repair-equivalence is defined
relative to a fixed loss and repair library.

\paragraph{Statistic-only adaptation can be insufficient.}
The proposition proves the statement for any statistic that aliases
margin-separated repairs. The constructive counterexample table makes the
condition explicit. Coarse passive statistics provide an empirical example. The
limitation is that richer passive vectors can be sufficient.

\paragraph{Diagnostic residuals can identify repair classes.}
The main sweep and probe ablations support this claim in the simulator. The
noise taxonomy limits it: the diagnostic coordinate fails when residuals are too
corrupted. The method should therefore be paired with probe reliability checks.

\paragraph{Mechanism labels require usable repairs.}
Repair-library ablations support this claim. When delay-brake is removed,
mechanism-first still diagnoses delay but cannot apply the intended action. The
resulting regret is not a classifier failure; it is a repair-library failure.

\paragraph{Out-of-library handling is unsolved.}
Friction is physically out-of-library but repair-equivalent to an in-library
response. Gain+delay is physically understandable but out-of-library as a repair
composition. Distance-based rejection is not enough to solve this distinction.

\section{Hardware Risk Cases Not Modeled}

The simulator excludes several hardware risks. Sensing delay and actuation delay
can interact, producing residuals that are not captured by the one-step actuation
model. Contacts can be spatially distributed, with torque, object rotation,
local deformation, and multiple contact patches. A probe that is safe at one
contact point can be unsafe at another. Soft objects can change during probing,
so the probe may alter the target domain rather than only observe it.

Repairs can also have hidden costs. Slip-safe control may increase cycle time.
Preload may damage fragile objects. Gain compensation may increase current draw.
Delay compensation can reduce responsiveness. A deployment loss should include
these costs if the repair is meant for hardware. Human safety constraints may
also prohibit exploratory contact. In collaborative settings, even low-amplitude
probes require an authorization layer.

\section{Probe Synthesis Directions}

The paper uses hand-designed probes to isolate the adaptation-coordinate
question. A next step is to synthesize probes by maximizing expected repair
information subject to safety constraints:
\[
\max_{\pi_p \in \Pi_{\mathrm{safe}}}
I(r^\star(d); \phi_{\pi_p}(d)) - \lambda C(\pi_p).
\]
This objective is intentionally about repairs, not full domain identification. A
probe that distinguishes two physical parameters with the same repair is less
useful than a probe that distinguishes two domains with incompatible repairs.

Probe synthesis should also include adversarial ambiguity search. Given a probe
set, search for pairs of domains with similar probe residuals and different best
repairs. Such pairs are the active-probe analogue of passive-aliasing
counterexamples. If they exist with nontrivial margins, the probe set is not
repair-sufficient.

\section{Why the Small Simulator Is Still Useful}

The simulator is intentionally small. It should not be used to claim broad
real-world performance. Its value is diagnostic: it isolates the coordinate
failure without perception, planning, or policy-learning confounds. It shows
that different mechanisms can share task-level outcomes, that the same repair
can cover many parameter values, that nuisance similarity can be misleading,
that partial system identification can estimate the wrong sufficient variable,
that active residuals can expose repair distinctions, and that missing repairs
and mixed mechanisms break a closed-library method.

These are design lessons rather than quantitative hardware predictions. A real
robot will need richer dynamics, force limits, uncertainty estimates, and safety
checks. The coordinate question remains unchanged: does the information used for
adaptation distinguish the repairs the robot must choose between?

\section{Extended Discussion of Repair Regret}

Repair regret is central because it measures the cost of choosing the wrong
repair. Success can saturate and mechanism-label accuracy can be misleading if
the label does not imply a useful action. Regret compares the selected repair to
the best repair available in the same library for the same domain.

In the main sweep, mechanism-first has zero measured regret because the labels
align with the best repair. Coarse passive statistics have nonzero regret
despite success 1.000. In repair-library ablations, regret reveals how much is
lost when the intended repair is missing. In unknown controls, regret separates
friction, where an in-library repair is adequate, from gain+delay, where the
closed library lacks a good single repair.

For hardware, regret is harder to measure because the best repair is not known
without trying alternatives. A practical audit can estimate regret on repeatable
fixtures, calibrated simulation, or safe counterfactual trials. If regret cannot
be measured directly, the system should report residual final error and
repair-switch outcomes after verification actions.

\section{Learned Representation Implications}

A learned representation can be repair-sufficient or repair-insufficient. If an
embedding maps two domains close together because they share appearance but they
require different repairs, it is repair-aliased. If it separates domains by best
repair, it is a valid mechanism-first coordinate even if it is not interpretable.

This suggests a representation-learning protocol: evaluate nearest-neighbor
repair accuracy and repair regret in the embedding space; search for close pairs
with different best repairs; train contrastive objectives where positives are
repair-equivalent domains and negatives are domains with incompatible repairs.
Such objectives may be more deployment-relevant than reconstructing every
simulator parameter.

\section{Domain Randomization Implications}

Domain randomization and mechanism-first adaptation answer different questions.
Randomization asks how to train a policy that tolerates variation.
Mechanism-first asks which repair to select when variation has produced a
deployment failure. They can be combined: a policy can be trained under broad
randomization while a deployment monitor uses probes to choose repair modes or
decide whether execution is safe.

Repair-equivalence can also guide randomization design. If many randomized
factors are repair-equivalent, the policy need not identify them separately. If
two factors produce domains with different best repairs but identical deployment
statistics, the training setup should include repair-relevant observations or
diagnostic interventions.

\section{Additional Hardware Audit Metrics}

A hardware audit should report more than success and final position. Useful
metrics include peak contact force, impulse, slip distance, object rotation,
time-to-completion, number of probe actions, energy, actuator current,
temperature, aborts, and post-repair residual error. The audit should also
report whether the selected repair changes the distribution of near misses.

For diagnostic probes, a confusion matrix should be weighted by repair regret.
Misclassifying gain as compliance may be harmless if both repairs are
conservative. Misclassifying delay as slip may be costly if it causes the
controller to react at the wrong time. The cost of a mistake matters more than
the name of the mistaken label.

\section{Compositional Mechanisms}

The gain+delay control is the clearest open problem. The target contains two
in-library physical mechanisms, but the repair library is not compositional.
Selecting gain compensation alone does not solve delay, and selecting
delay-brake alone does not fully solve gain loss. A compositional extension
would output a structured diagnosis such as
$\{\mathrm{gain},\mathrm{delay}\}$ and synthesize a combined repair. This is not
as simple as applying two repairs at once, because repairs can interact
nonlinearly.

\section{Unknown Detection Revisited}

Unknown detection is harder than classification. The rejection variant uses
probe-feature distance as a crude unknown score. That score is insufficient.
Friction is physically unknown but repair-equivalent to an in-library response,
so rejection is unnecessary. Gain+delay is near in-library components but
out-of-library as a repair composition, so distance can miss it. A better
unknown detector should estimate predicted repair regret or verify residual
improvement after a candidate repair.

\section{Final Artifact Acceptance Checklist}

The final paper is intended to be judged against explicit acceptance criteria.
First, the paper must state a narrow claim and avoid presenting simulator
evidence as hardware evidence. Second, the experiments must include positive
clean results, stronger passive baselines, nearest-neighbor baselines, partial
system-identification baselines, oracles, ablations, noise stress, and negative
controls. Third, the final manuscript must report final error and regret, not
only thresholded success. Fourth, the artifact must include generated figures,
tables, aggregate CSVs, a machine-readable summary, and a human-readable
summary. Fifth, the final PDF must be compiled from the expanded manuscript
after the full-scale artifacts are generated.

The paper now satisfies the first four criteria in the repository state. The
claim is explicitly bounded to a closed-library simulator. The evidence includes
main sweeps, probe-noise taxonomy, probe-set ablation, mechanism imbalance,
passive richness, scale sensitivity, repair ablation, unknown and mixed
mechanisms, threshold sensitivity, and formal counterexamples. The metrics
include success, final error, reward, regret, repair accuracy, mechanism
accuracy, and rejection rate where applicable. The generated files are stored in
the full-scale results directory and are used by the manuscript through figures

The remaining acceptance condition is external to the text: the final compiled
PDF must be copied to the download directory only after page count, text audit,

\section{Data Dictionary for Aggregate Rows}

Each aggregate row in the full-scale CSVs has a consistent interpretation. The
\texttt{suite} field names the experiment family, such as main sweep, probe
noise, or repair ablation. The \texttt{condition} field names the specific
stress condition, such as a noise type, a mechanism mix, a missing repair, or an
unknown mechanism. The \texttt{seed} field records the deterministic random seed
used for domain generation. The \texttt{train\_n} and \texttt{test\_n} fields
record the number of sampled training and test domains used by that row. These
counts differ across suites because the runner trades off breadth, nearest
neighbor cost, and the need for repeated seeds.

The \texttt{method} field names the adapter or oracle. The \texttt{n} field is
the number of test domains summarized by that row. \texttt{success\_rate} is the
fraction of domains whose final error is below the success threshold and whose
overshoot is within the allowed bound. \texttt{mean\_final\_error} is the mean
absolute distance from the target after the horizon. \texttt{p95\_final\_error}
is included for tail behavior. \texttt{mean\_reward} records the simulator
reward, which penalizes final error, action cost, and overshoot.

The regret columns compare the selected repair against the best repair in the
same finite repair library for the same domain. This is why repair ablation rows
can have low regret under the reduced library while still being worse than the
full-library main sweep. \texttt{repair\_accuracy} is the fraction of domains
where the selected repair equals the best-repair oracle. For methods that output
a mechanism label, \texttt{mechanism\_accuracy} compares the predicted mechanism
to the true in-library mechanism. In unknown controls, this value is
interpretable only for methods that still force an in-library label. The
\texttt{unknown\_reject\_rate} field records how often a rejection-capable method
returns an unknown label.

This dictionary is included because the CSVs are the authoritative evidence
artifact. The paper text reports selected aggregates, but the rows allow a
reviewer to recompute the means, inspect per-condition seeds, and check whether
a conclusion depends on one stress suite.

\section{Command Ledger and Execution Strategy}

The full-scale evidence was generated by a RAM-light two-stage workflow. The
runner \texttt{experiments/full\_scale\_mechanism\_first.py} can execute the
entire suite, but the final artifact uses suite-split execution so each
condition writes compact rows immediately. This avoids holding all raw episodes
or all suite outputs in memory at once. It also means that completed conditions
are preserved if a later stress test is interrupted.

The main sweep writes \texttt{main\_sweep.csv} and
\texttt{main\_by\_mechanism.csv}. Noise and probe ablations write single CSVs
because each suite completes quickly. Mechanism mixtures, scale sweeps, repair
ablations, unknown controls, and threshold sensitivity can be written as
condition-split files such as \texttt{mechanism\_mix\_gain\_heavy.csv} or
\texttt{repair\_ablation\_missing\_delay\_brake.csv}. The standalone summarizer
\texttt{scripts/summarize\_full\_scale\_results.py} reloads both base files and
condition parts, preferring condition parts when they exist. It then regenerates
the figures, TeX tables, JSON summary, and Markdown summary.

The important engineering decision is that raw traces are not the unit of
storage. The unit of storage is a compact aggregate with enough metadata to
audit the condition. This is appropriate for this simulator because the dynamics
are deterministic given a domain and repair, and because the paper's claims are
about repair-coordinate performance across sampled domains. If a later hardware
study uses stochastic rollouts, the same structure should be extended with
within-domain repeats and confidence intervals over repeated trials.

The execution strategy also separates evidence generation from manuscript
compilation. Figures are generated from CSVs, not hand copied from notebooks.
Tables are generated as TeX fragments. The manuscript includes those generated
fragments. This reduces the chance that a number in the paper drifts away from
the artifact on disk.
